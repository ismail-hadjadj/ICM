Initial Codebook v1
Qualitative analysis of structured classroom observation sheets
Approach: directed qualitative content analysis

Instructional_Format_and_Feedback
---------------------------------

Prompt_Activation
Definition: Teacher introduces the writing task, clarifies expectations, or activates prior knowledge.
Include when: Prompt explanation; task framing; defining key terms; activating prior knowledge.
Exclude when: Later guidance not related to task setup.
Example type of evidence: ‘Teacher spent 10 minutes explaining the prompt, defining key terms, and activating prior knowledge.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Helps explain whether implementation began with clear task orientation, which may support later writing performance.

Coded_Feedback_Used
Definition: Feedback is provided through coded symbols rather than direct written correction.
Include when: Coded symbols mentioned; drafts returned with codes; coding system referenced.
Exclude when: Direct written corrections or model answers are provided instead.
Example type of evidence: ‘Teacher collected drafts and applied coded symbols only, with no direct written correction.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Links qualitative evidence to the intervention mechanism behind experimental-group improvement.

Direct_Correction_Used
Definition: Teacher provides direct written correction, underlining, or model answers.
Include when: Red underlining; correct forms written above/beside errors; model text supplied.
Exclude when: Teacher prompts without giving the answer.
Example type of evidence: ‘Essays returned with red underlines and correct forms written above/beside errors.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Helps explain why the control condition may produce compliance but weaker learning transfer.

Revision_Cycle_Completed
Definition: The lesson includes a full sequence of draft, feedback, revision, and final version.
Include when: Revised/final drafts completed; feedback loop closed; presentation or reflection completes the cycle.
Exclude when: Revision attempted but not completed.
Example type of evidence: ‘All groups completed a final draft… Each group presented… students wrote individual reflections.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Supports interpretation of sustained improvement when students repeatedly revise their writing.


Student_Engagement
------------------

High_Engagement
Definition: Students actively attend to, process, or work with the task or feedback.
Include when: Engagement rated high/very high; active decoding, discussion, revision, presenting; strong involvement noted.
Exclude when: Mere compliance without active processing.
Example type of evidence: ‘Students decoded symbols with focus… estimated actively engaged students: 90–95%.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Helps explain stronger gains where students actively process feedback.

Low_or_Passive_Engagement
Definition: Students show minimal cognitive involvement, passive compliance, or superficial attention.
Include when: Students merely glance at corrections; compliance without engagement; explicit passivity.
Exclude when: Mixed or clearly active engagement.
Example type of evidence: ‘Students glanced briefly at corrections… classroom orderly but passive.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Supports explanation for weaker gains in the control group.

On_Task_Behaviour
Definition: Students remain behaviorally focused on lesson activities.
Include when: Orderly participation; groups remain on task; productive classroom behavior.
Exclude when: Evidence concerns only cognitive engagement level, not behavioral focus.
Example type of evidence: ‘All groups on task. Smooth transition. No off-task behaviour.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Distinguishes orderly participation from deeper engagement with learning.


Peer_Interaction_and_Collaboration
----------------------------------

Within_Group_Discussion
Definition: Students discuss corrections, meanings, or revisions within groups.
Include when: Group discussion; negotiation of corrections; collaborative drafting within groups.
Exclude when: Students sit together but work individually.
Example type of evidence: ‘Groups discussed error types and debated corrections.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: May explain how collaborative processing strengthened learning in the experimental group.

Across_Group_Exchange
Definition: Students exchange drafts, comments, or feedback across groups.
Include when: Inter-group exchange explicitly mentioned; groups comment on other groups’ work.
Exclude when: Only within-group collaboration occurs.
Example type of evidence: ‘Groups exchanged drafts with one another; students wrote brief peer comments.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Shows broader peer-feedback routines that may support revision and autonomy.

No_Peer_Interaction
Definition: Peer discussion or collaboration is absent.
Include when: ‘No peer interaction’ stated; silent individual work; no peer-learning facilitation.
Exclude when: Interaction exists, even if limited.
Example type of evidence: ‘No peer interaction. Students sat at tables for two in silence.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Helps explain why control-group correction may not translate into deeper learning.

Uneven_Participation
Definition: Collaboration exists but participation is not equally distributed.
Include when: Dominant speakers; quieter students passive; some group members contribute less.
Exclude when: Participation described as equitable.
Example type of evidence: ‘Some groups had dominant speakers; quieter students observed passively.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Useful for explaining variability within the experimental condition.


Metalinguistic_Use_and_Revision
-------------------------------

Metalinguistic_Use_Present
Definition: Students use grammatical or linguistic terminology to discuss errors or revisions.
Include when: Terms like tense, article, agreement, spelling, preposition; naming error types.
Exclude when: Corrections made without explicit linguistic terminology.
Example type of evidence: ‘Frequent use of “tense”, “agreement”, “preposition”, “article”.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Provides process evidence for improved linguistic awareness behind quantitative gains.

Metalinguistic_Use_Absent
Definition: Students do not use grammatical terminology or cannot name error categories.
Include when: No metalanguage observed; students cannot identify error types.
Exclude when: Any explicit linguistic terminology appears, even limited.
Example type of evidence: ‘No metalanguage used. Students could not name error types.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Helps explain weaker development in the control group.

Student_Initiated_Revision
Definition: Students revise or correct errors on their own or with peers, without depending on direct teacher correction.
Include when: Self-correction; independent identification and correction; peer-supported revision.
Exclude when: Teacher supplies the correction directly.
Example type of evidence: ‘Students identified and corrected errors without teacher prompting.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Directly supports interpretation of how the intervention improved writing outcomes.

No_Revision_Transfer
Definition: Students do not carry feedback into later writing or revision.
Include when: No revision attempted; copying model text; same errors persist; no transfer to own writing.
Exclude when: Partial but real revision occurs.
Example type of evidence: ‘No revision across six weeks… no transfer to own writing.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Explains why correction alone may not reduce errors quantitatively.


Teacher_Role
------------

Teacher_as_Facilitator
Definition: Teacher prompts, monitors, guides, or observes without supplying answers directly.
Include when: Encourages discussion; asks prompting questions; observes more than instructs.
Exclude when: Teacher directly corrects or answers.
Example type of evidence: ‘Teacher prompted: “What do your group members think?” No direct answers provided.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Supports the mechanism of learner autonomy and collaborative revision.

Teacher_as_Corrector
Definition: Teacher acts mainly as source of correction, answers, or model text.
Include when: Teacher fixes errors; provides answers; provides correct model version.
Exclude when: Guidance is indirect and dialogic.
Example type of evidence: ‘Teacher wrote a model text on the board. Students copied it.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Helps interpret the control condition as teacher-dependent rather than learner-driven.

Teacher_Prompting_without_Answer
Definition: Teacher intervenes through prompting rather than direct correction.
Include when: Teacher redirects students to peers; asks questions instead of giving answers.
Exclude when: Prompt is followed by the teacher giving the answer.
Example type of evidence: ‘Teacher prompted but did not provide direct answers.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Shows instructional support consistent with the intervention model.

Teacher_Confirmation_Dependency
Definition: Students rely on teacher validation before accepting or applying corrections.
Include when: Repeated ‘Is this correct?’; frequent need for teacher confirmation.
Exclude when: Students resolve issues mainly within groups or independently.
Example type of evidence: ‘Students frequently asked teacher “Is this correct?” before consulting peers.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Important for interpreting transitional stages of autonomy.


Constraints_and_Limitations
---------------------------

Symbol_Confusion
Definition: Students struggle to interpret coded feedback symbols.
Include when: Confusion with symbols; repeated need for reference cards; meanings not internalized.
Exclude when: Symbols clearly internalized and used automatically.
Example type of evidence: ‘Approximately one-third of students struggled to recall symbol meanings.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Explains early implementation friction in the experimental group.

Time_Management_Problem
Definition: Time is used inefficiently or lesson phases do not progress smoothly.
Include when: Delayed transitions; decoding/revision takes too long; phases not completed on time.
Exclude when: Timing is smooth and all phases are completed.
Example type of evidence: ‘Decoding and revision took longer than planned.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Helps qualify effectiveness by identifying implementation constraints.

Mechanical_Copying
Definition: Students copy a model text or correction without analysis or understanding.
Include when: Copying explicitly noted; model text reproduced rather than learned from.
Exclude when: Students adapt, revise, or produce original text.
Example type of evidence: ‘Students copied model text without analysis or questions.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Strong explanatory code for why direct correction may fail to improve writing.

Lack_of_Autonomy
Definition: Students depend heavily on teacher correction or do not self-correct.
Include when: Waiting for teacher; no self-correction; heavy dependence on teacher validation.
Exclude when: Students revise independently or mainly with peers.
Example type of evidence: ‘Students waited for teacher to correct errors.’
Likely relevance to quantitative findings: Helps explain weaker learning transfer and slower improvement.
